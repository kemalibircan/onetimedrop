## Why This Comparison Matters

QR and NFC both promise fast handoff with less typing, but they solve slightly different problems. NFC is tap-based and elegant when the hardware and software line up. QR is visible, universal, and easier to deploy across mixed devices.

For file transfer, compatibility matters more than novelty.

## Side-by-Side

| Option | Best part | Limitation | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| QR code | Works in any modern camera/browser flow | Needs camera scan | Mixed-device file transfer |
| NFC | Instant tap when supported | Hardware and permission dependence | Controlled environments |

## Why QR Usually Wins for File Transfer

A QR code can be shown on any screen and scanned by almost any phone. That makes it ideal for cross-platform workflows like [OneTimeDrop](/), where an iPhone, Android phone, Windows PC, and Mac all need a common starting point.

NFC is smoother in theory, but in practice it is less universal. Some devices support reading tags but not the same sharing flow, and many users do not know whether NFC is even enabled.

## When NFC Makes Sense

NFC is useful in fixed environments such as office badges, kiosk interactions, or a repeated internal workflow where every device is known ahead of time. For public-facing or mixed-device transfer, QR is easier to explain and support.

## FAQ

**Q: Is NFC faster than QR?**  
A: The tap itself can feel faster, but total workflow speed often favors QR because fewer compatibility questions get in the way.

**Q: Can QR transfer the file directly?**  
A: Usually the QR code opens the transfer session or file page. The actual transfer happens through the browser or service behind it.

**Q: Which is safer?**  
A: Safety depends more on the service, expiry rules, and data retention than on whether the connection started with a tap or a scan.
