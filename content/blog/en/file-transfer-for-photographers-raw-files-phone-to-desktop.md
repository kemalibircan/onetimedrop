## Why Photographers Need a Different Workflow

RAW files are large, important, and easy to mishandle if you treat them like ordinary images. The whole point of shooting RAW is preserving editing latitude, so a convenience-first share method defeats the purpose.

If you shoot or review on a phone, the desktop handoff needs to preserve the source file and naming discipline.

## Recommended Workflow

1. Export or locate the RAW files in the app or file manager that actually stores them.
2. Open [OneTimeDrop](/) on your desktop computer.
3. Transfer only the selects you need immediately, or batch the set carefully.
4. Confirm the file extensions and sizes after download.
5. Move them into your normal photo ingest folder and back them up.

## Why Not Use Chat or Social Tools?

Chat platforms are built for previews and fast sharing, not archival originals. Even if a service accepts the upload, it may create confusion between preview JPEGs and source RAW files.

## Quick Method Comparison

| Method | Best for | Advantage | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct browser transfer | Immediate selects | No install, no quality change | Manual workflow |
| Cable ingest | Full shoot archive | Strong control | More setup |
| Cloud sync | Multi-device editing | Remote access | Storage cost and sync delay |

## Workflow Tip

Name the session folder on your desktop immediately after download. The faster you move RAW files into your normal project structure, the less likely they are to get mixed with casual phone media.

## FAQ

**Q: Can phones really hold RAW files now?**  
A: Yes. Many camera apps and mobile editing tools store RAW or DNG files.

**Q: Should I convert to JPEG first?**  
A: Only if the destination use is delivery, not editing. Keep the RAW when quality matters.

**Q: What is the biggest mistake photographers make here?**  
A: Sending selects through messaging apps and later discovering only compressed versions survived.
